Kathryn McMahon

PhD Candidate, Department of Geography, University of California, Santa Barbara

I study how extreme heat, humidity, and air pollution shape human health, with a focus on prenatal exposure and child health in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. My work combines large secondary spatial datasets — gridded climate data, satellite-derived pollution estimates, and household health surveys — with quasi-experimental methods to isolate the causal effects of compound climate hazards. I'm increasingly interested in pairing this population-level work with individual-level, clinically grounded data, and am currently building a project with a federally qualified health center in California's Salinas Valley to study heat exposure and farmworker health.

I hold an MA in Geography from UC Santa Barbara and a BA in Geography & Global Studies from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. My PhD is supported by an NSF Graduate Research Fellowship and a UCSB Chancellor's Fellowship.

Research

Prenatal humid heat and child health

Dissertation · South Asia

Causal evidence that extreme heat combined with high humidity during pregnancy is substantially more damaging to child health outcomes than dry heat alone, with implications for child stunting as hot-humid extremes become more common under climate change.

Compound heat, humidity & air pollution

With Ann James, Simran Koul & Kathy Baylis

A high-resolution, near-global dataset linking humid heat, remotely-sensed air pollution, and infant mortality, paired with a causal machine-learning framework to estimate the compound effects of climate hazards that increasingly co-occur.

Extreme heat and farmworker health

With Kathy Baylis, Stuart Sweeney & Chris Funk · Salinas Valley, California

In partnership with Clínica de Salud del Valle de Salinas, a federally qualified health center serving roughly 50,000 patients, this project links individual-level clinic records with fine-scale heat, humidity, and air-quality exposure to estimate how climate hazards drive clinic visitation among agricultural workers.
